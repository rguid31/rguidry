The Journey That Shaped Me

My path wasn't linear, but every challenge taught me something valuable about resilience, adaptation, and the importance of asking "why."

2014-2023: The Engineering Odyssey

I arrived at LSU in fall 2014, ready to tackle chemical engineering. My high school years were packed with engineering prep: physics, drafting, AutoCAD, electronics, robotics. I chose chemical engineering because I was told the options were "limitless."

What I didn't expect was to spend more time navigating the system than actually learning in it. Over 140 credit hours, multiple internships, and countless hours of self-learning later, I realized my story wasn't unique.

"The challenge wasn't just the coursework—it was learning to advocate for myself while building the skills the curriculum couldn't provide."

What My Journey Taught Me

Self-Learning is Essential

I learned more from MIT OpenCourseWare, Khan Academy, and industry resources than from many traditional courses. The ability to teach yourself is the most valuable skill in engineering.

Networking Opens Doors

My internships with Georgia Pacific and Emerson Automation Solutions taught me that real-world experience and connections matter more than perfect grades.

Always Ask "Why"

Challenge unclear requirements and seek explanations. Respectful persistence often reveals the reasoning behind policies and helps you navigate systems more effectively.

Where Theory Meets Practice

ChemE lab time was one of the few moments where theory and practice truly met for me. Working with glassware, running experiments, and seeing chemical reactions in real-time—this was where engineering came alive.

But even in the lab, I noticed gaps. Louisiana is a top producer of sugarcane, and sugar refining is a major state industry. My family roots are in Galliano and Golden Meadow—places where sugarcane and coastal industries still shape daily life.

Yet in nine years of chemical engineering coursework, not a single class covered sugarcane processing. This taught me that curricula often miss opportunities to connect students to local industries.

How I Became a Data Analyst

My engineering background gave me a unique perspective on data. I learned to approach problems systematically, think critically about processes, and communicate complex technical concepts clearly.

The Pivot Point

After completing 131/139 credit hours towards a B.S. in Chemical Engineering, I made the conscious decision to switch to Mathematics with a secondary discipline in Chemical Engineering. This wasn't a failure—it was a strategic pivot toward my true passion: data-driven decision-making and the rapidly evolving fields of analytics, machine learning, and AI.

My experience with process optimization, statistical analysis, and cross-disciplinary problem-solving became the foundation for my career in data analytics. The engineering mindset of systematic thinking combined with the analytical skills I developed created a unique value proposition.

I've applied my analytical skills in both industrial and creative contexts—from process optimization projects at Georgia-Pacific and Cornerstone Chemical Company, to designing data-informed marketing campaigns. Completing the Google Data Analytics Professional Certificate solidified my technical toolkit, while my work as a Google Local Guide provided an avenue to practice these skills in real-world, user-focused projects.
